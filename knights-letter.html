Re: An invitation to found the Holy Trinity Caisse — a restoration of Father McGivney's charism, on a substrate his generation could not have imagined

Worthy Grand Knight,

I am writing to place before your council something Father McGivney would recognize.

In the basement of St. Mary's in New Haven in 1881, he gathered the men of his parish to answer a question his own family knew from the inside: what does the parish do when the wider economy will not care for a Catholic working family? His answer became the pass-the-hat, then the insurance benefit, then the Order you serve today.

Nineteen years later, six hundred miles north, my own grandfather helped run the caisse populaire — the parish credit cooperative founded by Alphonse Desjardins in Lévis in 1900 — that lent my father the money for his first car, his university, his family, and, in time, me. Two Catholic men, in two countries, in the same generation, saw the same wound of usury and answered it with the same instinct: when the wider economy fails a Catholic family, the parish becomes the economy.

I believe the time has come to plant that instinct again, in a third century, on a substrate their generation could not have imagined but would have blessed. And I believe Holy Trinity Parish is the right ground, and your council the right first witness.

What we are building

The Holy Trinity Caisse — a parish-scale mutual-aid economy with four lanes, each of which redirects an existing economic flow into the neighbor's grocery box.

Its constitutional rule is eight words:

One good deed. One witness. One hour. One token.

But its architecture is broader than the rule suggests. The caisse captures four separate flows the wider economy already extracts from Catholic households and redirects them into a single mutual-aid fund:

Lane 1 — Certified shopping. Any parishioner in good standing is certified by the parish and issued a code. From that moment forward, when they buy their ordinary groceries, diapers, tools, or school supplies through the caisse, they see and pay the same retail price they would at any store. But because the purchase is made under the parish's 501(c)(3) standing, the nonprofit spread — approximately 17% — flows to the caisse fund rather than to the state's tax till or Amazon's shareholders. Every certified household is a passive benefactor without writing a check.

Lane 2 — Deeds and donations. The traditional caisse populaire lane. A parishioner does an hour of service to a neighbor; one witness signs the deed into the ledger; one token is credited. The token does not expire, is transferable within the family and back to the parish, and cannot be traded or lent at interest — for that is the very usury this caisse exists to answer. And any donor moved by the works being done may contribute dollars to the fund, as your Knights have always done for the widows and orphans your Order was founded to serve.

Lane 3 — PAIX Witness. The deeds of the caisse are, with the consent of doer and neighbor, captured as short video witnesses and published across the parish's channels — on YouTube, on X, on Meta — under the shared imprint of PAIX Witness, a Catholic content platform designed to propagate the works of mercy to a wider world. Any revenue this public witness earns from the attention of the wider world — advertising, memberships, gifts of thanks — belongs to the caisse's fund, and to no private hand. What the world gives its attention to, the caisse converts to bread.

Lane 4 — Professional services. A parishioner who possesses a trade, a craft, or a profession may donate an hour of that trade to a fellow parishioner — the plumber a pipe repair, the attorney an estate plan, the accountant a tax return, the physician a housecall, the tutor an SAT prep session. The recipient owes nothing. The provider chooses their own measure — 0%, 10%, 20%, or up to 30% of the service's fair market value — and receives caisse credit at their chosen fraction, redeemable at the shop for their own household's needs. The professional's family does not go hungry because they gave their trade away. And the widow, the young family, or the elder receives at the market rate what the caisse alone could not afford to buy.

Why it works economically

For a Holy Trinity parish where 200 households route their ordinary spend through the caisse — spending they were already doing at Amazon or Walmart — and 20 professional parishioners each donate two hours a month at their trade, the caisse's first-year impact is approximately:

Lane Year 1 fund inflow
Certified shopping spread $20,660
Deeds and donations $12,000
PAIX Witness revenue $0 (builds to $10K–$50K by year 3)
Professional services (net to caisse) $57,600
Total year-1 fund inflow ~$90,000

Plus $72,000 of peer-to-peer professional value delivered — legal work, medical visits, plumbing repairs, and tutoring flowing between parishioners who never had to touch cash. Combined year-1 impact: approximately $162,000 for a 200-household pilot.

A pancake breakfast raising $10,000 delivers, at Walmart retail, about $9,150 of goods. The Holy Trinity Caisse's four lanes deliver, at year-1 scale, approximately 16 times the pancake-breakfast delivery for the same donor-effort input, once peer-to-peer professional value is included. It is not a better fundraiser. It is a structurally different economic instrument — one that captures continuous value from four flows the wider economy would otherwise extract, and returns it to the same neighbors your Order was founded to serve.

Every figure in this letter is drawn from published Amazon Business, YouTube Partner Program, and Kansas Department of Revenue sources, or from conservative assumptions about parishioner participation. The full one-page economic model is enclosed.

Why the ledger will be public

For a caisse that hides its ledger has already ceased to be one. Nine numbers — token velocity, fund solvency, procurement spread, deed diversity, household participation, video coverage, PAIX Witness revenue, professional services delivered, and average professional walk-away — will be published at a permanent public URL, refreshed nightly, verifiable by anyone. If those numbers hold for two quarters, PAIX will have proven its four-lane economic model in the open. If they do not hold, we will learn something honest and adjust before scaling.

What I am asking of your council

Not money. Not endorsement of a concept. Not a program integration.

I am asking your council to be the founding witness body of the Holy Trinity Caisse. Concretely, that means:

  1. Recognize the caisse as consistent with the founding charism of Father McGivney — the mutual-aid instinct that predates the insurance program and remains its animating spirit.

  2. Certify every willing Knight as a caisse shopper. Each Knight who routes his household's ordinary Amazon spend through the caisse becomes, without writing a check, a passive benefactor of the mutual-aid fund. A council of 100 Knights, each spending $75/month through the caisse, generates approximately $15,000/year of fund inflow through lane 1 alone. No fundraising work. No dinner. Just a code entered at checkout.

  3. Invite Knights with professional credentials to enroll as service providers in lane 4. Every Knight who is an attorney, CPA, physician, dentist, plumber, electrician, or tradesman can donate one to two hours a month of what they already do professionally, at fair market rate, in exchange for whatever caisse credit their household needs. A CPA Knight who donates one tax return a month at $500 market value, choosing a 20% walk-away, delivers $500 of legal help to a parish family and $100 of caisse credit to his own — while contributing $400 of net value to the caisse fund. Multiply by twenty professional Knights, and lane 4 alone runs to six figures a year.

  4. Nominate two or three Knights to serve as attesting witnesses. The "one witness" of the constitutional rule. Some deeds also become video witnesses on PAIX Witness — with the Knight's own name spoken aloud into the clip as attestor. Your Order becomes the visible, credible face of the caisse's public witness. A brother helping an elder into a car for dialysis, on video, with his name spoken by a fellow Knight — that is the propagation image PAIX Witness will build its channel on.

  5. Meet quarterly with the caisse's steward to review the public ledger and offer counsel, in the same spirit as a council reviewing its own charitable disbursements.

That is the entire first ask. No dollars committed. No council resources deployed beyond the time of two or three Knights willing to sign their name beside a deed, and the ordinary Amazon spending your Knights already do.

Five things I want to be honest about

Because a Knight deserves a real ask and not a polished one:

One — the professional-services lane needs counsel sign-off. The 501(c)(3) mechanic that lets a parish issue caisse credit to a professional provider at a bounded fraction of market value is defensible under settled US tax law, but the specific walk-away cap (currently proposed at 30%, aligning with the combined federal and Kansas marginal tax rate) and the IRS receipt language should be blessed by Kansas jurisdiction tax counsel or by the Knights of Columbus legal counsel before the caisse enrolls its first professional. This is cheap and easy insurance, and the Knights' fifty years of parish-scale mutual-aid experience are exactly the right place to seek it.

Two — PAIX Witness monetization takes 6–18 months. YouTube, X, and Meta all require threshold volumes before revenue starts. Lane 3 is $0 in the year-1 baseline. Any revenue there is upside, not core to the model. The caisse's year-1 viability rests on lanes 1, 2, and 4.

Three — the affiliate rate is 1–10% until we negotiate the full nonprofit-procurement stack. Standard Amazon Associates pays 1–10% depending on category. The higher 14–22% figures elsewhere in this packet assume Amazon Business nonprofit purchasing, ATEP sales-tax exemption, and cooperative-purchasing contracts stacked together — which require the parish to charter its 501(c)(3) instrument first and negotiate its Amazon Business account second. Until those are done, communicate 1–10% as the honest range. The 17% figure is the target for the mature caisse, not the day-one number.

Four — there is also a proposed PAIX code for non-parishioner shoppers. Anyone — Catholic or not, parishioner or not — could route their Amazon spend through a network-level code, with the flowback landing in PAIX (the shared umbrella under which every caisse is chartered) rather than in any single parish. There is no legal restriction on non-Catholics supporting a Catholic-affiliated 501(c)(3) instrument in the United States. The PAIX entity has not been chartered yet and is a design proposal, not a program. Details in the enclosed PAIX page.

Five — a single scandal can kill the video lane permanently. A minor filmed without guardian consent, a beneficiary shamed in a clip, a demonetization event — any of these can zero out lane 3 and damage the caisse's reputation across all four lanes. The evidence protocol enclosed with this letter defines what is never on video, and the steward is not merely a certifier but a standards editor with a veto. The Knights' judgment on this, once you have read the protocol, will be gratefully received.

If, after four quarters, the nine public metrics hold and your council is satisfied that the caisse is what it claims to be, the conversation can widen — to matching-fund programs, to Kansas jurisdiction adoption, to the offering of the template to other Catholic parishes across the country.

But that later conversation must be earned by the ledger, not requested in this letter.
