The Charter of the Holy Trinity Caisse

The parish's economic engine. Built by the Knights, maintained by the stewards, used by every working group of the parish — the education and formation groups, the social-development groups, the healthcare professionals, the trades, the young, the elders — each contributing what they have to contribute. In time, the ledger will show who is doing the most.

A mutual fund for the works of mercy, kept by the parish, denominated in trust.

The Rule

One good deed. One witness. One hour. One token.

The Six Articles

I. Of the Deed. A token is minted only by an act of service freely given to another — the sick visited, the hungry fed, the ignorant taught, the stranger welcomed, the elder driven, the child watched, the grieving accompanied. No token is minted by promise, by need, or by pity. The deed is the deposit slip.

II. Of the Witness. Every deed is entered into the ledger by one witness who saw it and who signs their name beside it, first in the flesh, and now also on the video record. The witness speaks their name aloud into the clip, and the beneficiary consents to be seen or is protected from view — for the witness is not a judge but a neighbor, and the neighbor's dignity is not for sale. One name beside one name, as it has always been done among a people who know each other. The video is witness for the century that has forgotten how to keep its word.

III. Of the Hour. The unit of the caisse is one hour of service. An hour of prayer with the dying is worth an hour of framing a wall is worth an hour of tutoring a child. The caisse does not price the soul of a labor; it counts the giving of a life. What is earned in an hour is spent at par.

IV. Of the Token. The token does not expire, for a good deed does not expire. The token may pass within a family, and it may be returned to the parish, but it is not traded, sold, or lent at interest — for that is the very usury this caisse exists to answer. What the parishioner has earned, the parishioner or their household may spend, on any good the shop carries, for as long as the caisse endures.

V. Of Certification. Any parishioner in good standing may be certified by the parish and issued a code by which their ordinary purchases from the shop shall be made in the parish's name. Certification is a welcome, not an interview: the parishioner gives their name and their phone number, a steward confirms them against the parish roll, and the code is theirs. The certified parishioner sees the same retail price a stranger would see, and pays it in full, that no one may say the caisse subsidizes its own. But the stewardship spread the parish's collective standing has earned upon that purchase — the tax the state would have taken, the discount the vendor would have withheld, the rebate the vendor would have quietly pocketed — is thereby directed into the common fund, to the aid of those who have earned tokens by their deeds. What the certified parishioner would have spent anyway becomes, without extra cost, an act of mercy. There is no charity in this arrangement, only stewardship of what the wider world had already decided to concede to a Catholic parish, and which the parish now redirects to its own.

VI. Of Professional Service. A parishioner who possesses a trade, a craft, or a profession may offer its exercise as their gift to the caisse. The plumber may donate the mending of a pipe; the attorney the drafting of a will; the tutor an hour of learning; the physician a visit to the housebound; the accountant a season's tax return. Every such service is delivered to its recipient at its full market rate, and the recipient owes nothing. The parishioner-professional, in offering the service, chooses their own measure — zero, that the gift may be whole; or a fraction, that their own household may also be provided for as they provide for another. The caisse acknowledges the gift at its market rate, issues the professional caisse credit at their chosen fraction, and retains the balance as service rendered to the mission. The hour of a professional is honored at its market price; the professional's walk-away is their own conscience's answer to what their household can spare. No parishioner is paid a wage; every parishioner is provided for; and no professional's family goes hungry because they gave their trade away.

The Five Flows

The caisse is one fund with five lanes, each of which feeds or spends it:

  1. Certifying — the parishioner is welcomed to the shop and given their code. From that moment forward, every ordinary purchase they make through the caisse — at full retail, as any shopper would pay — quietly directs the stewardship spread into the common fund.
  2. Earning — the parishioner does the deed; the witness signs; the token is credited at one token per hour.
  3. Serving — the parishioner-professional performs an hour of their trade, donated to the caisse; the recipient or a steward witnesses; the service is delivered to its recipient at full market rate, and caisse credit is issued to the provider at their chosen walk-away fraction of that rate; the remainder is booked as the parish's own gain.
  4. Giving — the donor, moved by the works being done, contributes dollars to the pooled fund, or sponsors an account in the name of another. Receipts are issued as the law provides.
  5. Spending — the parishioner draws upon their account, in tokens or in credit or in dollars, for goods they need: food, clothing, tools, transport, medicine, learning. The shop settles with the vendor from the pool.

The token is the claim of the hour given. The credit is the claim of the trade given, at the giver's own measure. The dollar is the settlement. The deed is the deposit. The ordinary purchase is the tithe. The neighbor is the bank.

The Order

The caisse is kept under the roof of Holy Trinity Parish, under the authority of its pastor, under the umbrella of PAIX, and in the memory of two men who saw, in their own century, what usury does to a people and what mutual aid can restore: Alphonse Desjardins, who founded the first caisse populaire in Lévis in 1900, and Father Michael J. McGivney, who founded the Knights of Columbus in New Haven in 1882. This caisse is not a new thing. It is their thing, kept.

The Fathers' Work, Continued

We do not question the structure our Fathers put in place. The Church has her hierarchy, her sacraments, and her orders, given by Christ and kept by His Apostles' successors; the parish has her pastor; the Knights have their Order; the caisse populaire has its steward and its ledger. We do not reorganize what has been given to us. We do not modernize the offices. We do not add new categories the Fathers did not know.

We simply take the technology of our own century — the ledger that fits on a phone, the shop that fits on a website, the witness that fits on a video — and we put it to the use our Fathers had in mind from the beginning: that no Catholic family should go hungry while the parish's own economic standing is being spent on strangers, and that every parishioner should be able to serve his or her neighbor within the structure the Church has always kept.

This is the whole ambition of the caisse. Not to invent. To continue.

The Posture

The caisse does not take rent. It does not collect tax. It does not arbitrate between parishioners, or decide who among them is the winner and who the loser. It does not sell its ledger, its attention, or its neighbors. It does not race yachts. It exists to help the people who need help, and to support the institution that founded, in its time, all the other institutions the wider world now runs.

What the caisse is not, it need not say aloud. It is enough that it does not become those things, and that its ledger, being public, will show that it did not.

The Promise

The caisse will publish, quarterly and in the open:

  • the number of parishioners certified and shopping,
  • the number of tokens earned and by how many hands,
  • the number of tokens spent and on what,
  • the dollars given and by whom (as donors permit),
  • the dollars paid to vendors, and how much the parish's stewardship spread returned to the fund.

For a caisse that hides its ledger has already ceased to be one.

The Public Witness

The deeds of the caisse are the caisse's public witness. With the consent of doer and neighbor, they are captured as short video and published across the parish's channels — on YouTube, on X, on Meta, and on such other surfaces as the stewards deem worthy — under the shared imprint of PAIX Witness, the content platform by which many parishes may propagate the works of Catholic charity into a wider world. That the works of mercy done in Lenexa may be seen, taken heart from, and imitated in Wichita, in Winnipeg, and in Manila.

Any revenue that this public witness earns from the attention of the wider world — advertising, memberships, gifts of thanks, on any platform now or hereafter — belongs to the caisse's fund, and to no private hand. The parishioners in the clips are not paid, the videographers are not paid, the stewards are not paid; but the neighbor who could not otherwise have been served is fed, and clothed, and driven, and taught. What the world gives its attention to, the caisse converts to bread.

This is the third turn of a wheel that Alphonse Desjardins turned first — capturing the savings flow of a Catholic people and redirecting it to their own — and Father McGivney turned second — capturing the insurance flow of a Catholic people and redirecting it likewise. PAIX Witness turns the third: capturing the attention flow that the world now spends on short-form video, and redirecting it, too, to the neighbor. The three flows, once separate, are now one caisse.
